The big picture

One mental model carries the whole site: everything is a different projection of the same two objects — a table of 5,097 passages and a matrix of 5,097 "meaning vectors" (one embedding per passage). The pipeline below runs once, on a laptop; the site itself is static files. Two of the five pages don't even ship precomputed answers — the affinities and distinctives pages compute their statistics in your browser, at load time, from the same JSON the map uses.

1The corpus — from books to passages

pipeline/chunk.py turns the public-domain source texts into uniform rows. Each text has its own parser (the Analects' numbered books, the Quran's suras, Proverbs' verses…), but all of them emit the same record: tradition · source · ref · text. Three rules make passages comparable:

  • Max ~700 characters — longer passages split at sentence boundaries, so one dot never represents a whole chapter.
  • Min 60 characters — fragments merge forward into the next passage.
  • Cleanup — Gutenberg italics markers, editorial footnotes […], "&c.", and dash variants are normalised away so the models never see them.

Result: 5,097 passages across fifteen traditions — Stoicism 759, Hebrew wisdom 685, Confucianism 578, Islam 518, Buddhism 497, Hinduism 399, Taoism 361, Christianity 360, Zoroastrianism 239, Jainism 199, Bahá'í 175, Sikhism 144, Epicureanism 67, Mesopotamia 59, Ancient Egypt 57. These sizes matter later: every statistic on the affinities and distinctives pages is normalised so big traditions can't win by volume alone.

Most texts come from Project Gutenberg as clean plain text a regex parser can split. A few — like the Zoroastrian Gāthās — exist only as archive.org OCR scans, where the translation is broken across page-break footnote columns and running heads. Those go through an extra step, pipeline/ocr_extract.py, which asks Claude Opus to pull just the numbered verses back out (stitching split lines, dropping the apparatus) before chunking; a canonical verse-count check flags anything the pass dropped or doubled.

Licensing — why every source is free to use

The corpus is built entirely from public-domain material, and deliberately so: the underlying scriptures are ancient, and each specific translation here is a public-domain edition — most from the 19th or early 20th century, a few older still (Meric Casaubon's Meditations dates to 1634) — whose copyright has long since lapsed (their translators — James Legge, J. M. Rodwell, Meric Casaubon, L. H. Mills and their contemporaries — all died a century or more ago, so the texts are public domain under both the US pre-1929 rule and the life-plus-70 rule most of the world uses). Nothing here relies on a modern copyrighted translation.

Two practical notes on how that public-domain status flows through the pipeline:

  • Project Gutenberg. A Gutenberg file is public-domain text wrapped in a trademark license: the License governs redistribution under the "Project Gutenberg" name and its header/footer boilerplate, not the transcribed work itself. chunk.py strips that boilerplate and keeps only the bare public-domain text, so nothing on this site carries the Gutenberg trademark or its terms.
  • The archive.org scans. A faithful reproduction of a public-domain book adds no new copyright, so the scanned Sacred Books of the East (Mills 1887, Jacobi 1895), Macauliffe's Sikh Religion (1909) and the Babylonian & Assyrian Literature (1901) are as free to use as any Gutenberg text — the OCR step just cleans them, it doesn't relicense them.

Two honest caveats. "Public domain" is a copyright status, not a claim about translation quality or canonical standing — these are the freely-available editions, not necessarily the best modern ones, and that choice shapes what the map can see. And the site never reproduces a whole work: each passage is a short excerpt shown alongside its machine-written gist, well within fair use even were any source still in copyright.

2The distillation — why an LLM is in the loop

The first version of the map embedded raw text only, and it failed in an instructive way: embeddings latched onto vocabulary, not ideas. Every "The Master said…" clustered with every other one; "love/hate" passages grouped by the words themselves. So every passage is first distilled by Claude Opus into one plain modern-English sentence — the claim, stripped of speaker framing, archaic wording, and metaphor:

Matthew 6:24 — "No man can serve two masters: for either he will hate the one, and love the other…"
→ gist: "You can't serve both God and money equally; choose one as your primary loyalty."

Mechanics: batches of 60 passages per claude -p call, 2 parallel workers, every call's cost read from the CLI's JSON output and summed, a probe batch first, and a budget stop. Gists are cached in gists.json keyed by a hash of the passage text, so re-runs only pay for new passages. Total spent distilling the current corpus with Opus: ~$22.

3The embedding — one meaning vector per passage

Both the original text and the gist are embedded with BAAI/bge-large-en-v1.5 (1,024 dimensions), then blended. The gist dominates — it carries the idea — but the original text keeps enough weight that imagery and register still count for something:

flowchart LR
  T["original passage<br/>“No man can serve two masters…”"]:::file --> ET["bge-large<br/>1,024-d vector"]:::step
  G["Opus gist<br/>“You can't serve both God and money…”"]:::file --> EG["bge-large<br/>1,024-d vector"]:::step
  ET --> B["blend<br/>0.35 × text + 0.65 × gist"]:::step
  EG --> B
  B --> V["renormalise to length 1<br/>= the passage's meaning vector"]:::file
  classDef file fill:@FILEBG,stroke:@FILEBR,color:@INK,stroke-width:1.5px
  classDef step fill:@STEPBG,stroke:@STEPBR,color:@INK
Vectors are unit length, so "similarity" everywhere on this site is the cosine of the angle between two vectors — computed as a plain dot product.

Embeddings are cached as .npy files keyed by a hash of model + input, so every later stage (ideas, search) reuses them for free. The map, the ideas clustering, and the neighbour lists all sit on this same matrix — that's what makes the views mutually consistent.

4The projection — positions, neighbours, resonance

embed_project.py derives the three things data.json ships per passage:

  • x, y — UMAP squeezes 1,024 dimensions to 2 (cosine metric, n_neighbors=25, min_dist=0.08, fixed seed). Distances on the map are suggestive, not exact — UMAP preserves neighbourhoods, not global geometry. That's why the precise statistics below never use x/y.
  • nb — the passage's top-10 cosine neighbours with scores, computed in the full 1,024-d space. These are the threads the map draws, and the only input the affinities page needs.
  • r (resonance) — how many other traditions appear among those 10 neighbours: 0 = the passage is an island (speaks only to its own tradition), 8 = nearly everyone echoes it.

The five pages — what each one actually computes

The map (index.html)

The most direct view: every passage drawn at its UMAP position, coloured by tradition. Click a dot → the panel shows its text, gist, resonance, and its nb list split into "echoes in other traditions" vs "within its own". No further math happens on this page — it renders stage 4 as-is.

Ask the Ages (the search box on the map)

The problem: the corpus lives in bge-large's 1,024-d space, but a 1.3 GB model can't run in a visitor's browser, and a query embedded by a different model lands in a different space where cosine is meaningless. The fix is a second, smaller space used only for search:

Same model on both sides = same space. Verified: the browser path reproduces the Python pipeline's rankings with scores within ±0.004 (the tiny gap is int8 quantisation + the q8 ONNX build). $0 per query, no server, no query leaves the page.

Two honest costs of this design: search quality is bge-small's, slightly below the map's bge-large (only search is affected), and the model download is the site's one external request — hence the consent prompt.

The ideas (ideas.html)

ideas.py runs k-means (k=60, fixed seed) over the same meaning vectors. Each cluster becomes a bubble:

  CL --> LY["bubble layout: area ∝ passages,<br/>seeded at the cluster's mean map<br/>position, overlaps relaxed apart"]:::step
  CO --> PG["THE IDEAS page"]:::page
  LB --> PG
  LY --> PG

  classDef step fill:@STEPBG,stroke:@STEPBR,color:@INK

  classDef page fill:@PAGEBG,stroke:@PAGEBR,color:@INK,stroke-width:1.5px

The "most shared" ranking uses eff — the effective number of traditions: exp(entropy) of the cluster's tradition shares. Intuition: a cluster split 50/50 between two traditions scores exactly 2.0; spread evenly across all fifteen it would score 15.0; one passage from a fifteenth tradition barely moves it. It measures even spread, not just presence.

"Freedom through releasing craving" — 108 passages: 47 Buddhist, 31 Hindu, then a scattering of Jain, Sikh, Taoist, Islamic and Confucian. Touches eleven traditions, but Buddhism and Hinduism dominate → eff = 5.0, honestly closer to "five even traditions" than eleven.

The affinities (affinities.html)

Computed in your browser from the neighbour lists, in about a millisecond. The question: when a passage from tradition A looks at its 10 nearest neighbours, how often does it see tradition B — compared to what chance would predict from B's size alone?

flowchart LR
  A["Christianity's 360 passages<br/>× 10 neighbour slots<br/>= 3,600 looks outward"]:::file --> O["observed:<br/>563 land on Islam"]:::step
  N["Islam is 518 of 5,096<br/>other passages ≈ 10.2%"]:::file --> E["expected by chance:<br/>3,600 × 10.2% ≈ 366"]:::step
  O --> L["lift = 563 / 366 = 1.54"]:::page
  E --> L
  L --> SYM["symmetrised cell =<br/>mean of both directions<br/>(1.54 + 1.02) / 2 = 1.28"]:::page

  classDef page fill:@PAGEBG,stroke:@PAGEBR,color:@INK,stroke-width:1.5px
A real worked example — the strongest cell among the major traditions. Note the asymmetry the mean hides (hover any cell to see it): Christian passages reach toward the Quran markedly more (1.54) than Quranic passages reach back (1.02 — essentially chance), since the far larger Islamic corpus has more of its own to echo.

Lift 1.0 = exactly chance. The normalisation is the whole point: without it, 759-passage Stoicism would dominate every row simply by being everywhere. Two things then surface on their own. First, the largest cells belong to the smallest traditions, where a handful of shared-theme neighbours swings the ratio — Bahá'í↔Mesopotamia (2.71), Ancient Egypt↔Hebrew wisdom (2.28, the very Amenemope→Proverbs borrowing the lineage traces) and Mesopotamia↔Zoroastrianism (2.04) top the matrix. Second, among the pairs with a genuine family tie the affinity rises well above chance regardless of size: Hinduism↔Sikhism (1.65), Buddhism↔Jainism (1.50, the two śramaṇa movements), Confucianism↔Taoism (1.30), Christianity↔Islam (1.28) and Epicureanism↔Stoicism (1.23), with Islam↔Zoroastrianism (1.06) and Buddhism↔Hinduism (1.05) just above it — Buddhist passages lean Hindu (1.25), though Hindu passages lean back toward their own metaphysics (0.84). Nothing in the pipeline knows any history; these convergences re-emerge from meaning alone.

Clicking a cell ranks the actual passage pairs behind the number (every A-passage's neighbour links into B, deduplicated, sorted by cosine) — so the summary statistic is always one click from its raw evidence.

The distinctives (distinctives.html)

The inverse question: which clusters does one tradition dominate beyond its size? Also computed at page load. For each tradition T and cluster c (where T has ≥ 3 passages):

  • purity = T's share of the cluster ("47 of 108 passages").
  • lift = purity ÷ T's share of the whole corpus, so small voices can rank.
  • ideas are ranked by purity × lift; deliberately no hard purity cut-off — at k=60 Epicureanism's best cluster is only 19% pure (it's 1.3% of the corpus, so that's still 14× its share) and a 70% bar would erase the smallest traditions entirely. Instead the purity is printed plainly on every row.
  • each card also shows the island share — the % of passages with resonance 0. It spans a wide range: Epicureanism, Christianity and Ancient Egypt 0%, Hebrew wisdom and Stoicism 9%.

The lineage (lineage.html)

The odd one out, on purpose. It is not derived from embeddings at all: ~30 major scriptures placed left-to-right by date, coloured by language family, with descent and influence edges — every node, edge, date, and "what flows along this edge" note written and sourced by hand (each node links a reference). It answers the historical question the other views deliberately ignore: the map shows that traditions converge; the lineage shows which of those convergences have a family explanation — and, by elimination, which ones are the genuinely independent arrivals.

Caveats & costs

  • Everything is corpus-relative. "Distinctly Hindu" means "rare in the other fourteen text selections here", not "absent from human thought". The Christianity sample is the New Testament's teaching chapters, which likely tilts its affinities toward Hebrew wisdom.
  • Two AI layers sit between you and the text: the gists (which steer the embeddings) and the cluster names. Both are always shown next to the original passage, never instead of it.
  • Reproducible by construction: fixed random seeds, content-hash caches, and the map's x/y reused by the ideas layout, so re-runs don't shuffle the site.
  • Total LLM spend: ~$31 (distillation ~$22, OCR verse-extraction ~$7, cluster naming ~$2, plus a few cents for the Gāthās OCR pass). The embeddings, projections, affinities, distinctives, and every search query: $0.
  • An interpretive curiosity, not a theological claim.
